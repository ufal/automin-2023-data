SUMMARY
- PERSON251 expresses satisfaction with the good relations between the Council's Conventional Arms Exports Working Group, COARM, and the European Parliament's Subcommittee on Security and Defence.
- The EU Code of Conduct on Arms exports was adopted in 1998.
- The code is not legally binding, but Member States have a political commitment to abide by its provisions.
- Slovenian Presidency is working for its adoption in the first half of this year.
- The European Commission has a clear interest in the effective control and monitoring of EU arms exports.
- The Commission took advantage of its chairmanship of the Kimberley Process last year to push for the development of controls to prevent and deter the use of diamonds to fund arms purchases by rebel groups.
- The European Council has dragged its feet on transforming the EU code of conduct on arms transfer into a legally binding document.
- There are currently estimated 400 companies producing arms in EU Member States.
- Parliament's motion expresses its frustration at the current deadlock on the EU Code of Conduct on Arms Exports.
- It is 10 years since the EU agreed a code of conduct for arms exports, but it has not yet been transformed into an effective instrument for controlling arms exports by all EU companies and governments.
- 7 out of 10 of the world's top arms-exporting countries are EU Member States.
- The EU finds itself in the incomprehensible position of supplying arms and fuelling conflicts in the very countries it is pouring development aid.
- PERSON255 is rapporteur for the control of arms exports from the European Union.
- He wants to see the current Code of Conduct transformed into a more efficient and effective instrument that ensures greater and better controls of EU exports from EU territory and by EU companies.
- A few countries are opposed to such a step, notably France.
- The EU and its Member States have become the world's number one arms exporter.
- PERSON259 calls on EU Member States to adopt the EU Code of Conduct on Arms Transfers.
- The Lisbon Treaty strengthens the role of the EU as a global player.
- He wants the Council to give a plausible explanation why the adoption of this Common Position has been delayed.
